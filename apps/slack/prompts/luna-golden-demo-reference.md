# Golden reference film — study the craft, never the surface

`inputs/references/golden-demo/**` contains the **complete editable source and
rendered visual evidence for one strong, hand-authored launch film for a
different product (Slack).** It is supplied so
you are not authoring blind: it shows what a high-craft, seekable, multi-act
film actually looks like in code. It is a *craft* reference, not a template and
not a shot list.

## What to extract (the reason it works)

- **Multi-act structure.** It is edited into distinct acts that tile one
  timeline, not a single tableau that slowly accretes panels. Contrast between
  acts is where the energy comes from.
- **Dynamic range.** It swings deliberately — bright fields against dark
  product scenes, quiet holds against loud statement type, abstract shapes
  against real UI. One register for the whole film reads as flat.
- **Frame-filling composition.** Hero beats fill the frame edge to edge; type
  reaches display scale when it carries the message. Large dead margins around
  small centered content read as an unfinished wireframe.
- **One energy peak.** Connective motion stays subordinate to a single
  dominant moment, then the film settles into a genuine resting hold.
- **Causal continuity.** Important states arise from what just happened — an
  object is carried across a boundary, a click causes a visible consequence, a
  shared environment persists — or a hard cut changes register for a reason.
- **A resolved payoff.** It lands on a committed closing statement, not a
  diagram left mid-thought.

Read `STORYBOARD.md` for the beat-by-beat intent, then `index.html`,
`style.css`, `polish.css`, `config.js`, and the paused seekable master in
`timeline.js` to see how those choices are built. Inspect
`rendered/contact-sheet.jpg` and `rendered/temporal-strip.jpg` to see the actual
film across time, and use `rendered/qa-report.json` only as evidence that the
reference itself passed its deterministic frame checks.

## The fence — do NOT copy the surface

This film is Slack's, for Slack's story. Invent a treatment specific to the
product in your fact envelope. **Never reuse** its palette, colors, copy,
brand marks, logo, wallpaper, product-UI layouts, shot order, act count,
typewriter device, camera moves, or logo behavior. Do not make your film look
like this one. Reusing its appearance is a failure, not a success. Extract the
principles; leave the pixels.
